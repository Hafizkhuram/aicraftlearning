When to trust AI output (and when not to)

The fastest way to lose a client is to send them AI output you didn't check.

You've now got a mental model of how AI works, the context window, and how to write a proper prompt. The last piece of the puzzle is judgement. Specifically: for any given piece of AI output, how much of it should you trust, and where exactly do you need to step in?

Get this right and AI makes you dramatically faster without making you sloppier. Get it wrong and you'll either waste time double-checking everything, or — worse — confidently ship something that was never true.

!
The common misconception

"If the AI sounds sure, it's probably right." Covered this in Lesson 2.1, but it bears repeating because it causes more real-world damage than any other misconception. Confidence and correctness are separate properties. Treating them as the same is how professionals end up apologising to clients.

The trust hierarchy

Not all AI output sits at the same risk level. Some of it you can use as-is, some you should skim before sending, some you should fact-check line by line. The trick is knowing which is which before you look at the output — because the output itself always looks equally polished.

Here's the hierarchy. Three tiers, based on the type of task, not the type of tool.

✓

High trust

Language work

Drafts, rewrites, summaries of content you provide, tone changes, brainstorms, structured thinking, translation.

Use, lightly edit
~

Medium trust

Reasoning and analysis

Arguments, comparisons, recommendations, pros and cons, explaining concepts. Usually sound, but occasionally wrong in subtle ways.

Read critically
!

Low trust

Specific facts, numbers, citations

Names, dates, statistics, URLs, academic citations, prices, live data, exact quotes. This is where fabrication happens.

Verify, always

The hierarchy maps directly to how LLMs work. High-trust tasks are where language patterns are strong and stable — the model is doing exactly what it was built for. Low-trust tasks are where you need exact correctness the model can't guarantee because prediction isn't precision.

1. The task: "Rewrite this three-paragraph supplier email in a warmer tone."

"Hi Sam — hope you're well. Just wanted to touch base about last week's shipment. I know delays happen, but we've been waiting longer than usual and it's starting to affect our restocking plan. Any update on a revised timeline would genuinely help…"

High trust — use and lightly edit A pure language task: tone rewrite on content you provided. The model is doing what it's best at. Skim for your voice, tweak a phrase, ship.

2. The task: "Which three competitors had the highest market share in UK meal-kit delivery in Q3 last year, with exact percentages?"

"HelloFresh led with 42.3%, followed by Gousto at 28.1% and Mindful Chef at 9.4%. The top three together accounted for 79.8% of the UK market in Q3."

Low trust — verify every figure Specific market-share percentages, to one decimal place, from last year. The shape is textbook hallucination territory. The names may be real; the numbers are almost certainly invented to fit the pattern. Treat as a starting point for your own research, never as a source.

3. The task: "My shop's taking £4K a week but we're breaking even. What are three likely reasons, and what should I look at?"

"Three likely culprits worth checking: thin margins hidden inside a few high-volume SKUs (check gross margin per product line); creeping fixed costs, particularly rent and card-processing fees; and labour scheduling mismatched to actual footfall. I'd start with a simple margin analysis…"

Medium trust — read critically This is reasoning about your business. The categories are sensible and well-structured, and it points you at real things to look at. But it doesn't know your actual business, so it may have missed the thing that really matters (seasonality, a bad supplier, staff turnover). Treat it as useful analysis from someone who doesn't know your details.

4. The task: "Give me three academic studies about four-day work weeks, with citations."

"Haldane & Fitzgerald (2019), 'Productivity outcomes in shortened work weeks,' Journal of Organisational Psychology, 34(4), pp. 412–429. Also Rahim et al. (2021)…"

Low trust — verify before citing Academic citations are the classic case of AI hallucination. Author names, journal titles, page numbers all follow a familiar shape, so the model produces that shape — whether the paper exists or not. If you ever need real sources, use a search tool or library, not an LLM on its own.

Three tells that you're in low-trust territory

Good judgement isn't about catching every error. It's about recognising the shape of a task that's likely to go wrong before you've even looked at the output. Three tells, any one of which should slow you down.

1

Specific numbers you didn't provide

Percentages, currency figures, precise dates, exact word or item counts. If you didn't give the model the raw data, it's guessing.

2

Names, titles, URLs, citations

Anything that could be looked up and verified externally. These are the easiest things for the model to invent and the easiest for you to catch if you check.

3

Anything "as of today" or "current"

The model doesn't know what today is unless you tell it. Live prices, current leaders, recent events — none of it is reliable without a search tool attached.

4

The output reads like Wikipedia

Confident, factual-sounding, detailed paragraphs about people, places, or companies. That's the classic hallucination voice. Check anything falsifiable.

The one thing to remember

AI is your fastest intern, not your advisor. Use accordingly.

Drafts, summaries, rewrites — let the intern run. Numbers, names, and final decisions — those are still yours.

A simple rule for deciding where to step in

When an AI output lands in front of you, ask yourself one question: "What would it cost me if a single detail in this were wrong?"

If the answer is "almost nothing" — it's a first draft of an internal note, a brainstorm, a rough email you'll edit anyway — ship it, edit lightly, move on.

If the answer is "a client relationship, my credibility, or real money" — stop. Check the numbers. Verify the names. Re-read the claims. That five minutes of checking is the 20% of work from the 80/20 frame in Lesson 1.3. It's also the difference between a professional who uses AI and a professional embarrassed by AI.

This is the habit that compounds. Every time you check something and catch an invented fact, you're also training your instinct for when to check next time. After a few weeks, it becomes automatic — you'll feel the low-trust tasks before you see the output.
