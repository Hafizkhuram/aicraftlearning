Drafting at scale — with your voice

You need ten LinkedIn posts, a month of newsletter drafts, and outreach for fifty prospects — all sounding like you wrote them. Generic AI slop won't do. Here's the pattern that makes scale and voice genuinely compatible.

This is where most people are quietly disappointed by AI content. You ask Claude to write a LinkedIn post, and it produces something competent, confident, and instantly recognisable as written by an AI. "I'm excited to share." "Let's unpack this." "Here's the thing." You can't publish it. You end up rewriting so much that you may as well have started from scratch.

The fix isn't a better prompt. The fix is teaching Claude what your writing sounds like, then letting it draft from that starting point. Done right, Claude becomes a ghost-writer who has read everything you've published — which is exactly the kind of ghost-writer you'd hire if you could.

!
The common misconception

"AI content always sounds like AI content." Only if you use it generically. Give Claude five to ten examples of your actual writing and tell it to match the voice — and the output stops sounding like ChatGPT and starts sounding like a first draft you'd recognise as yours. The trick isn't in the prompt, it's in what you feed Claude alongside it.

The fourth shape of bulk work

Module 3 has now taught you three shapes of multi-file work. Here's the fourth, which this lesson adds:

  • 3.1 — Many files, one transformation. Structured extraction. Invoices, contracts.
  • 3.2 — One file, many transformations. Cleanup and reporting.
  • 3.3 — Many files, synthesis. Research across sources.
  • 3.4 — Source material, generated content. Examples teach a voice; Claude drafts new things in it.

The new shape inverts the usual relationship. In the first three lessons, the files were work to do. Here they're examples to learn from. That's the shift — and once you see it, you'll recognise voice-matching as the generalisable pattern for all AI-assisted writing, not just social posts.

Generic output vs. your voice

Here's a concrete before-and-after, for a freelance brand strategist posting on LinkedIn about "why most rebrands fail." Same prompt. The only difference is whether Claude has access to her writing samples:

Without samples

Generic AI default

"Let's unpack one of the biggest traps in branding today. Most rebrands fail — not because the new logo isn't beautiful, but because the strategy underneath isn't aligned. Here's the thing: rebranding isn't a visual exercise, it's a strategic one. What do you think?"
With 7 samples

In her voice

"Nine out of ten rebrands don't fail because the logo is wrong. They fail because nobody asked 'what actually has to be different now?' The mark changes. The strategy doesn't. Six months later, everyone's back to the old behaviours with a nicer colour palette. Real rebrands are behavioural, not visual."

The second one is specific. It's opinionated. It has a point of view. It doesn't open with "Let's unpack" or close with "What do you think?" — because Priya never writes that way. Claude learned the voice from reading her samples, and drafted accordingly.

Note: it's still a draft. Priya will still edit. But she's editing 85% of the way there, not 10% of the way there. That's the difference between "this is a productivity tool" and "I gave up because it all sounded like a robot."

The three ingredients

Every voice-matched drafting workflow needs the same three things. Miss any one and the output reverts to generic AI-speak.

Part 1

A voice folder

5-10 pieces of your actual writing. Newsletters, posts, emails, blog drafts — anything published in your voice. Stored in /reference/voice/.

Part 2

A CLAUDE.md rule

One line instructing Claude to read the voice folder before drafting anything, and to match the patterns it sees there — cadence, tone, opinions, signatures.

Part 3

A task-focused prompt

What to draft, what it's about, any constraints. No voice instructions in the prompt — those belong in the sample folder and CLAUDE.md.

The split matters. Task-specific things go in the prompt. Voice — the thing that stays constant across every piece you ever write — goes in CLAUDE.md and the sample folder. Trying to cram voice into every prompt is a mistake: you'll forget half of it, and Claude will interpret the other half differently every time.

Setting up the folder

The folder structure is a small variation on what you learned in Lesson 2.1. The only new addition is /reference/voice/ — a subfolder Claude reads at the start of every drafting session:

📁 ~/Projects/Content/ 📄 CLAUDE.md 📁 reference/ 📁 voice/ # 7-10 pieces of your writing newsletter-2026-01.md newsletter-2026-02.md linkedin-march-1.md linkedin-march-2.md blog-rebrand-post.md ... 2-5 more 📁 briefs/ # what you want drafted 📁 drafts/ # where Claude writes output

What goes in the voice folder

You want Claude to see a representative range of your writing — not just your best work, but a mix of formats and lengths. Seven to ten pieces is plenty. Quality matters more than quantity: one strong sample beats five mediocre ones.

  • A few newsletters or blog posts (long-form cadence)
  • Two or three social posts (short-form punch)
  • Maybe one email you're proud of (conversational register)
  • Nothing from years ago when you were writing differently

Delete or update the folder as your voice evolves. You're essentially maintaining a lightweight portfolio that tells Claude who you are as a writer, right now.

The CLAUDE.md for content work

Here's the relevant section of a content project's CLAUDE.md. The key line is the one about reading the voice folder before drafting:

📄 CLAUDE.md
# Content project ## About me I'm Priya, a freelance brand strategist. I write for small and medium business clients — mostly UK, professional services. ## How I write - British English always - Short paragraphs. Opinionated. Direct. - I don't open with "let's unpack" or "here's the thing" - I don't close with rhetorical questions ("what do you think?") - I sign LinkedIn posts with no sign-off at all - Newsletter sign-off is "— Priya" with an em-dash ## Before drafting anything new Read all files in /reference/voice/ first. Match the cadence, sentence length, level of opinion, and sign-offs you see there. If my draft output doesn't sound like a first draft I could recognise as mine, you've missed the voice. ## Avoid - Corporate filler ("leverage", "going forward", "unpack") - Exclamation marks - Opening with a question - Vague hedging ("many people think", "some would argue")

The "before drafting anything new" instruction is the load-bearing one. It turns the voice folder from decoration into an active input. Every single draft Claude produces gets weighed against the samples first.

The drafting prompt

With voice handled in CLAUDE.md and the sample folder, your prompts get dramatically shorter. You're no longer describing how to write — you're describing what to write about:

Claude Code · Content project
> Draft 5 LinkedIn posts for the next two weeks. Themes: 1. Why most rebrands fail (behavioural, not visual) 2. The "alignment meeting" as the cheapest strategic move 3. A client story: how we killed a tagline 4. The difference between positioning and messaging 5. Three questions I ask on every discovery call Length: 120-180 words each. No hashtags. Save to /drafts/linkedin-2026-may-1.md with H2 for each post. Match the voice in /reference/voice/ as always.

Three hundred words of prompt gets you five posts, each in Priya's voice, ready for a light edit. Do this Monday morning and LinkedIn is sorted until payday.

The compounding here is real. Your first content project takes twenty minutes to set up — curating the voice folder, writing the CLAUDE.md. Every drafting session after that takes two minutes of prompting. A month in, you're producing more content than you used to, in less time, and it actually sounds like you. The voice folder quietly becomes one of the most valuable digital assets you have.

Isn't this cheating?

Addressing the obvious question

Let's be honest about what this is and isn't.

This pattern is not "AI writing your posts for you." It's AI producing a first draft in your voice, which you then edit with your actual judgement. The thinking — what opinion to express, what example to use, what you actually believe — is still yours. The typing and the voice-matching is what Claude handles.

That's no different in kind from hiring a good ghost-writer. The only difference is speed, cost, and the fact that the ghost-writer has read everything you've ever published. Used this way, Claude produces content that's better than your rushed first draft, in a fraction of the time. Used badly — publishing unread, unedited AI output — you get the slop everyone complains about.

The edit at the end is non-negotiable. If you skip it, this is a publishing liability, not a productivity win.

Where this pattern fits

Voice-matched drafting isn't just for social posts. The same voice folder plus a different brief gets you:

  • Cold outreach at scale — draft 30 prospect emails from a list of targets. Each tailored, each in your voice.
  • Newsletter drafts for the quarter — give Claude the themes, let it draft openings, edit to taste.
  • Client update emails — a batch of weekly updates across your active clients, all in your actual voice rather than AI-corporate.
  • First drafts of proposals — the structural draft, in your voice, for you to sharpen. Skip the blank page entirely.
  • Internal memos or team comms — consistent voice across your company's writing, even when several people are contributing.

Every one of these is the same workflow: voice folder + CLAUDE.md + task-focused brief. Scale without sounding like a bot.

What to watch out for

Too few samples, or the wrong ones. Two samples isn't enough — Claude has barely anything to triangulate from. Fifteen old samples from before your voice settled can actively mislead. Seven current, representative pieces is the sweet spot.

Trying to describe the voice in words instead of showing it. Descriptions ("write in a conversational, direct, warm tone") are weak signals. Actual examples are strong signals. A sentence of yours in the samples is worth a paragraph of tone description in the prompt.

Publishing the first draft. Even with a good voice folder, Claude occasionally gets a sentence wrong — a phrase you'd never use, a claim that's not quite yours. Always do a read-through and edit. Think of the draft as a 15-minute ghostwriter handoff, not a finished piece.

Letting the voice folder go stale. Your writing evolves. Revisit the voice folder every quarter. Delete pieces that no longer represent you. Add new ones that do. The folder should always reflect your current voice, not who you were eighteen months ago.

The one thing to remember

Voice comes from samples, not from prompts. Seven pieces of your writing beats seven paragraphs of voice description.

Curate once, draft forever. The compounding value of a well-maintained voice folder is genuinely underrated.
