Claude Code: Setup, Workflow & Mastery Real business workflows

Research workflows — from question to answer in minutes

You've got 14 PDFs, a folder of article transcripts, and your own notes. Your client wants a briefing paper by Friday. This is the lesson where "a week's worth of research" becomes an afternoon's worth — safely.

Research is the workflow that most feels like it shouldn't be automatable. Surely reading 15 things and forming a view is the bit a human has to do? And yet this is where Claude Code delivers some of its biggest time savings — as long as you use it in a specific way, with one specific safety rule.

We're going to build up to that rule. First, let's see where research synthesis sits in the pattern taxonomy you've been learning.

!
The common misconception

"I'll just ask Claude what's in these documents and trust the answer." This is where research workflows quietly go wrong. Unlike extraction (where Claude finds specific things) or cleanup (where there's a right answer), synthesis asks Claude to form a view across many sources. That view can be plausible-sounding but not actually grounded in the sources — unless you structure the prompt to require it to be.

The third pattern in Module 3

You've now seen the first two workflow shapes in this module. Here's where today's lesson fits in the taxonomy:

Lesson 3.1

Many files, one transformation

Extract the same fields from each file, combine into a structured output. Invoices, contracts.

Lesson 3.2

One file, many transformations

Apply a checklist of cleanup rules to a messy file and generate a clean version plus a report.

Lesson 3.3 — this one

Many files, one synthesis

Read varied sources and produce a combined understanding — a briefing, a themes document, a view.

Extraction (3.1) is pulling. Cleanup (3.2) is normalising. Synthesis (3.3) is combining. The shift from extraction to synthesis is the moment Claude Code starts doing work that used to feel like genuine thinking — and it's also the moment a specific new kind of risk shows up, which we'll get to.

The worked example

You're a consultant. A client — a mid-sized UK software company — has asked you to brief their board on "the current state of AI adoption in financial services." They want a 1,500-word briefing paper in three days. In a normal world, that's three days of reading, note-taking, and drafting.

Over the last couple of weeks you've been gathering sources. They now live in a project folder like this:

📁 ~/Projects/AI-Financial-Services-Brief/ 📄 CLAUDE.md # project setup and citation rules 📁 sources/ # varied source material bcg-ai-banking-report-2025.pdf mckinsey-gen-ai-fs-2025.pdf ft-feature-ai-trading.pdf oxford-ai-regulation-paper.pdf monzo-tech-blog-post.md hsbc-investor-day-notes.md ... 8 more sources 📁 outputs/ # where Claude writes the briefing 📁 reference/ # client context client-context.md

Fourteen varied sources. Some are 60-page consulting reports. Some are blog posts. Some are your own shorthand notes from a conference. The job: cross-read them and produce a coherent client briefing.

Why synthesis is different from extraction

Before we write the prompt, it's worth pausing on why research needs more care than the workflows in 3.1 and 3.2. A quick contrast:

The synthesis risk

Claude can make things up when synthesising — but not when extracting

If you ask Claude to extract the amount from an invoice, it either finds the number or tells you it can't. There's a verifiable right answer. But if you ask Claude to summarise the state of AI in banking across 14 reports, it's synthesising — forming a view — and that view can contain confident-sounding statements that aren't actually grounded in the sources. Plausible, but not real.

This is the single most important thing to understand about research workflows. It's not that Claude is unreliable — it's that synthesis is a fundamentally different kind of task from the ones you've done so far. Left alone, synthesis can confidently produce "McKinsey found that 78% of banks are investing in AI" when McKinsey actually said something quite different, or didn't say anything at all in that report.

Good news: there's a simple structural fix. It's the most important prompting move in this lesson.

The citation-required rule

The rule

Every claim in the output must point back to a specific source

Tell Claude, in the prompt and in your CLAUDE.md, that no claim in the synthesis should appear without a citation to the source document it came from. No unsourced generalisations. No "industry experts say…" without a named expert. No statistics without the report they came from.

This single rule transforms Claude's synthesis from "trust me" to "here's the evidence trail." It also gives you an obvious check: any claim without a citation is either mistaken or invented, and you can spot-check them in about a minute.

In practice, this means asking for claims to be formatted like (source: bcg-ai-banking-report-2025.pdf, p.12) or with an inline source tag. The exact format doesn't matter — what matters is that every factual sentence has one.

The CLAUDE.md

Before the main prompt, the research project's CLAUDE.md sets up the conventions. This is what turns one-off synthesis into a repeatable research workflow:

📄 CLAUDE.md
# AI in Financial Services — client briefing project ## About this project I'm producing a board-level briefing for a mid-sized UK software company on AI adoption in financial services. Sources are in /sources/. Final deliverable is a 1,500-word briefing paper. ## Citation rules (important) - Every factual claim must cite its source — use the format (source: <filename>, p.X) after each claim - Do not add claims that aren't grounded in the sources - If sources disagree on a point, show both views and name them - If I ask something the sources don't cover, say so — don't fill in ## Tone and structure - British English. Sentence case headings - Written for board members (strategic, not technical) - Short paragraphs. Numbers and specifics, not vague generalities - UK-centric framing where sources allow ## When in doubt - If a source is ambiguous, quote it directly rather than paraphrase - List any gaps in evidence in a separate /outputs/gaps.md

The first section of that file — the citation rules — is the safety harness. With it in place, every synthesis you run in this project inherits the "show your work" requirement, and you never have to re-type it in a prompt.

The research prompt

Now the actual synthesis request. Notice it's still inputs/work/outputs shaped, but the work has a specific synthesis structure and the outputs include a grounding layer:

Claude Code · AI-Financial-Services-Brief project
> Read every file in /sources/ (14 varied documents — reports, articles, notes). Also read /reference/client-context.md. For a board-level briefing paper on "the state of AI adoption in financial services", identify: 1. The 3-4 most important themes across the sources 2. For each theme: what the sources actually say, with citations 3. Where sources disagree — and who's saying what 4. Two or three genuine surprises worth highlighting to the board 5. Any notable gaps where the sources don't cover the question Write the result to /outputs/briefing-draft.md as a 1,500-word paper structured around those themes. Every factual claim must include a citation in the format (source: <filename>) as specified in CLAUDE.md. Also write /outputs/sources-map.md listing each source with a one-line summary — so I can verify the briefing against them.

Three things worth noticing about that prompt:

It asks for themes, not "everything in the sources." A good synthesis is structured around 3-5 ideas, not a bullet list of everything interesting. Naming the target shape forces Claude to be selective in the same way a good human analyst would.

It asks for disagreement explicitly. This is the sharpest test of real synthesis. Disagreement between sources is often the most useful thing to surface — and it's also where lazy synthesis smooths over reality. Asking for it by name gets you the nuance.

It asks for "surprises" and "gaps". Surprises are what make the briefing feel like real thinking; gaps are what make it honest. A briefing that names what it doesn't know is much more trustworthy than one that sounds comprehensive.

What good output looks like

Here's a trimmed section of what Claude produces. Note the inline citations on every claim — that's the rule in action:

📄 briefing-draft.md (extract)
# AI in Financial Services — Board Briefing Four themes · 14 sources reviewed · March 2026 ## 1. Deployment is accelerating but unevenly Large incumbent banks have moved from AI experimentation to production deployment in the last eighteen months (source: bcg-ai-banking-report-2025.pdf, p.8). HSBC reported over 200 AI use cases in live operation at their most recent investor day (source: hsbc-investor-day-notes.md), and Monzo has moved internal operations tooling onto AI agents for tier-one support (source: monzo-tech-blog-post.md). Smaller challenger banks and building societies have not kept pace. McKinsey found that only 23% of UK financial services firms below £1bn in assets have deployed generative AI in production (source: mckinsey-gen-ai-fs-2025.pdf, p.14). ## 2. Regulation is the most-cited blocker Sources disagree on severity. The BCG report frames UK FCA guidance as "workable for incumbents" (source: bcg-ai-banking-report-2025.pdf, p.22), while the Oxford policy paper argues the current framework leaves smaller firms exposed to "disproportionate compliance overhead" (source: oxford-ai-regulation-paper.pdf, p.6). Surprise worth flagging: both sources agree that UK regulation is clearer than EU AI Act provisions for the sector — a competitive tailwind the board may not be aware of (source: oxford-ai-regulation-paper.pdf, p.11; bcg-ai-banking-report-2025.pdf, p.24). ## Gaps in the source set - Limited UK-specific data on productivity gains from AI agents - No source covers the impact on frontline retail banking staff - Nothing from challenger bank leadership on strategic positioning

Every factual statement has a source in parentheses. The "sources disagree" section is a feature, not a weakness — it's the honest nuance your client actually needs. The "gaps" section at the bottom tells you what you still need to go find.

Your job after Claude's done

And here's the other half of research workflow discipline: the output isn't the final deliverable. It's a well-grounded draft that you now verify and edit.

Concretely, here's what you do after Claude produces the briefing:

  1. Spot-check the citations. Pick three or four of the highest-stakes claims and open the source file at the cited page or section. Do the quotes match? Is the framing accurate? Five minutes of checking, massive trust buyback.
  2. Read the "gaps" section first. If the gaps listed are ones you can fill from other sources, do so. If they're ones your client needs to know about, keep them in.
  3. Edit for your own voice. Claude wrote a competent draft; your job is to make it sound like you wrote it. This is where your CLAUDE.md voice rules help, but a final pass by you is still part of the workflow.
  4. Remove the inline citations before sending. The citations were your trust mechanism. The client doesn't need to see every filename — just keep a cited version for your own records, and send a clean version to the board.

This post-processing is maybe twenty to thirty minutes. Compared to reading 14 sources from scratch, it's a night-and-day saving.

Manual time vs. Claude time

Numbers on a real synthesis task like this:

Manual

~3 days

Read each source. Take notes. Find themes. Draft. Revise. A full chunk of a consulting week.

Claude Code

~90 minutes

Set up the folder (15). Run the synthesis (15). Verify and edit (60). Client-ready draft in an afternoon.

Research is the workflow that looks most like "real thinking" — and it's also the one that compresses the most. Weeks become afternoons. Afternoons become sandwiches.

Reuse beats novelty. The citation-required rule, the "themes + disagreement + surprises + gaps" prompt structure, and the sources-map sidecar are all portable. Save them into your CLAUDE.md for any research project and every future briefing uses the same trustworthy pattern. You're not writing a new prompt each time — you're running a recipe.

What to watch out for

Three specific things that trip up first-time research workflows.

Mixing very short and very long sources in one prompt. A 60-page PDF and a 200-word blog post will both influence the synthesis, but the shorter source can get under-weighted. If one source is disproportionately important, flag it in the prompt ("give extra weight to the Oxford policy paper for regulatory questions").

Ambiguous "the state of X" questions. If your brief is too broad, the synthesis will be too thin. Narrow the scope — "state of AI adoption in retail banking" is much more useful than "AI in finance." Claude can only synthesise as sharply as your question lets it.

Not checking the sources-map. If the one-line summary of a source doesn't match what you remember the source saying, that's a yellow flag. Check that source first when you spot-check. It's the cheapest way to catch a synthesis that's drifted.

The one thing to remember

Research synthesis is fast and powerful — but every claim must cite its source. That one rule is the whole difference between trustworthy research and plausible fiction.

Put it in your CLAUDE.md. Repeat it in every prompt. Spot-check three citations before trusting the output. Do this, and weeks of reading compress into afternoons.
