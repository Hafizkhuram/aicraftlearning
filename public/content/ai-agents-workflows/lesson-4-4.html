Your 30-day plan — making it stick

Most people who finish a course like this close the browser and never open n8n again. This lesson exists so you're not one of them. What follows is four weeks of small, specific, daily moves — by day 30, you've got real systems running, not a head full of diagrams.

You've just finished fifteen lessons on how to build workflows and agents that do real work. Three builds are (mostly) ready to go — email triage, competitive intelligence, meeting-to-Notion. You know when an agent architecture earns its place and when a plain workflow is the honest answer. You know how to keep these things running over time. Knowing all that and never using it is the default outcome for most course graduates. This is the lesson designed to stop that happening.

What follows is a month-long plan, built around 15 minutes a day. That's the dose that survives actual working life. Any more and you skip days; any less and nothing compounds. Four weeks in, you'll have three to five systems running, a weekly review rhythm, and — the real goal — the reflex of reaching for n8n when something in your week is repeated, rule-shaped, and low-stakes.

!
The common misconception

"I'll block a whole Saturday next weekend and properly get stuck in." Almost nobody does this, and those who try once rarely do it twice. Tools absorb through small, daily contact — not heroic weekend sessions. Fifteen minutes repeated twenty-five times beats four hours repeated never.

The shape of the plan

The four weeks each apply a piece of what you've just learned:

  • Week 1 — Ship what you built. Get the three course builds from draft into actually running on your real work. Apply the hardening from Lessons 2.4, 3.4, and 4.3.
  • Week 2 — Your fourth system. Pick one recurring task from your real work and build it using the trigger-extract-route-notify pattern from Lesson 4.1. Don't invent; reuse.
  • Week 3 — Your fifth system. Build a second original. By now you've got the shape, and it takes half the time.
  • Week 4 — Make it durable. Set the three calendar reminders from Lesson 4.3. Run your first monthly review. Retire anything that shouldn't survive the test.

The day labels below are guidance, not rules. If a Tuesday disappears under a client emergency, roll it to Wednesday. What matters is that most days get fifteen minutes on the current week's focus.

Week 1 — Ship what you built

Week 1

Move the three course builds into real use

By Sunday Three workflows active on real inboxes, real calendars, real Drive folders — not toy data. Kill-switches in place, run log spreadsheet capturing output, weekly review calendar reminder set.
  1. Day 1 (Mon)

    Point the email triage build at your real inbox. Change the Gmail Trigger from the test label to your live inbox (or a narrower filter you trust). Drafts-only mode — no auto-send. Run once; read the first five drafts carefully.

  2. Day 2 (Tue)

    Hook up the run log sheet. Lesson 2.4 pattern: every execution writes a row with timestamp, email subject, classification, draft length. This is how you'll notice drift next month.

  3. Day 3 (Wed)

    Point the competitive intelligence agent at real targets. Update the competitors list in the Edit Fields node. Run once manually — read the full trace in Executions. Check the cost. If it's over £1 per run, tighten the prompt before scheduling.

  4. Day 4 (Thu)

    Schedule the competitive intelligence run weekly, Monday 7am. Confirm the £50 monthly cap from Lesson 3.4 is still in place in the Anthropic Console. This is your first autonomous thing.

  5. Day 5 (Fri)

    Point the meeting-to-Notion build at your real Drive folder. The one where your transcripts actually land. Confirm the Notion database is correctly shared with the integration. Run against last week's meeting as a test.

  6. Weekend

    Set the weekly review reminder from Lesson 4.3 — Friday 4pm, 15 minutes, "Check n8n executions + review drafts." This is the single most important calendar event of the whole month.

Refresh from: Lesson 2.4 (hardening Build #1), Lesson 3.4 (hardening Build #2), Lesson 4.1 (Build #3), Lesson 4.3 (weekly review rhythm).

Week 2 — Your fourth system

Week 2

Build a fourth — don't invent, reuse the pattern

By Sunday One new system built with the trigger-extract-route-notify pattern, running in drafts-only mode, solving a real problem you actually had this week.
  1. Day 8 (Mon)

    Pick the task. Look at last week's to-do list. Which repeated task ate the most time? Invoice processing, customer enquiry tagging, support ticket routing, content idea capture — any of those work. Pick one where mistakes are recoverable.

  2. Day 9 (Tue)

    Fit it to the pattern. What's the trigger (email arriving? file appearing? schedule?), the extract (what does the LLM pull out?), the route (where does structured data go?), the notify (what do you get told?). If you can't fill in all four boxes, you haven't scoped it clearly enough.

  3. Day 10 (Wed)

    Build the trigger + extract. Same shape as Build #3: trigger node → file/content fetch → Anthropic node with temperature: 0.1, JSON output. Test with one real input. Read the JSON carefully — is it extracting what you actually need?

  4. Day 11 (Thu)

    Add the route + notify. Where does the structured output go — Notion, Airtable, a spreadsheet, a draft somewhere? Add that node. Finish with a notification node (Gmail summary to yourself) so you know it ran.

  5. Day 12 (Fri)

    Run it on five real inputs. Not toy data. Real. Note what it got right, what it got wrong, what edge case broke it. Fix one thing, not three.

  6. Weekend

    Decide: leave it manual (you trigger runs yourself) or activate the trigger. For the first fortnight, manual is usually the right call. There's no prize for autonomy on day 13.

Refresh from: Lesson 4.1 (the four-stage pattern), Lesson 3.2 (prompt engineering for extraction), Lesson 2.4 (drafts-only safety).

Week 3 — Your fifth system

Week 3

Go again — faster this time

By Sunday A fifth system running. Five total. The pattern feels familiar, the time-per-build is dropping, and you're reaching for n8n before you reach for doing things manually.
  1. Day 15 (Mon)

    Pick the next task. Same test as last week: repeated, rule-shaped, recoverable. Do you need memory across runs? If yes, revisit Lesson 3.3 before starting.

  2. Day 16 (Tue)

    Run the deconstruct test from Lesson 4.2. Is this genuinely a multi-agent problem, or a single agent with the right tools? Almost always the second. Pick single-agent-plus-tools unless you genuinely can't see how one model could hold the whole job.

  3. Day 17 (Wed)

    Build the core pipeline. Trigger → extract → route → notify, same as Week 2. You should notice the setup goes faster. That's the compound effect — you're not learning n8n, you're using it.

  4. Day 18 (Thu)

    Add guardrails. From Lesson 3.4: max iterations, timeout, cost cap if it's an agent. Run log if it's customer-facing. Kill-switch that you can flip in one click.

  5. Day 19 (Fri)

    Test on ten real inputs. This time you're pattern-matching failures. Is there one kind of input that consistently breaks it? Fix that one kind; accept that the long tail is a long tail.

  6. Weekend

    Optional sixth system. Only if you're enjoying this and have real demand. Don't force volume — five systems that actually run beats eight systems that got abandoned.

Refresh from: Lesson 4.2 (single vs multi-agent deconstruct), Lesson 3.3 (when memory matters), Lesson 3.4 (hardening an agent).

Week 4 — Make it durable

Week 4

Reviews, retirements, and the systems that survive

By Sunday Three calendar reminders live (weekly, monthly, quarterly). One monthly review completed. One workflow retired because it didn't earn its keep. You're now running a portfolio, not a pile of one-offs.
  1. Day 22 (Mon)

    Set the three reminders. From Lesson 4.3: Friday 4pm weekly (15 min — executions + drafts), first-of-month 9am monthly (30 min — costs + OAuth + provider news), first of quarter (1 hour — prompt review + model version tests).

  2. Day 23 (Tue)

    Run your first monthly review early. Yes, all five workflows. Check Executions tab. Check Anthropic Console monthly cost. Note the number. This is your baseline for "is it getting more expensive" next month.

  3. Day 24 (Wed)

    Apply the retirement test. For each of the five systems: if I were starting from scratch today, would I build this? If the honest answer is no for any of them, retire it. Archive the workflow, don't leave it dormant. Dormant things break quietly.

  4. Day 25 (Thu)

    Document what's running. A simple page — Notion, a Google Doc, a text file. Each system gets a name, a one-line purpose, where the output goes, the kill-switch location, and the monthly cost. Future-you will thank present-you.

  5. Day 26 (Fri)

    Measure time saved. Pick your best-performing build. Roughly, how much time would those tasks have taken you manually over the last month? Write that number somewhere. This is the case you'll make to yourself on the day you almost quit.

  6. Weekend

    Rest. The system maintains itself from here — three calendar reminders and a portfolio of durable builds. Day 30 is not the end. It's the point at which "maintain" replaces "build" as the dominant verb.

Refresh from: Lesson 4.3 (weekly/monthly/quarterly cadences + retirement test), Module 4 review (the whole picture).

The daily rhythm

What 15 minutes looks like

The small, repeatable shape

Every daily task above fits the same rough shape. Open n8n (1 minute). Look at what the day calls for (2 minutes). Do the thing (10 minutes). Look at the execution trace, note what worked and what didn't (2 minutes).

Do this before the working day starts, or at the natural pause after lunch, or in the last 15 minutes before you close the laptop. What matters is that it happens, not when.

When a day goes sideways

The bad-day protocol

Three rules for days you nearly quit

Some days you won't have 15 minutes. Some weeks will be awful. The difference between people who build a habit and people who don't isn't motivation — it's a light-touch recovery plan. These are the rules:

  1. Missed a day? Fine. Pick up where you left off, not where the calendar says you should be. The plan moves, not you.
  2. Missed three days? Drop to the minimum viable action — open n8n, look at the executions tab, close. Restore momentum in 60 seconds. Tomorrow you build again.
  3. Missed a whole week? That week's focus becomes next week's focus. Don't try to catch up — it's not a race, it's a habit. The calendar waits.

What you'll have by day 30

End state

A month from now, you'll have all of this.

  • ✓
    Three to five systems running on your real work — some manual-trigger, at least one on a schedule, all with kill-switches and run logs.
  • ✓
    A weekly review rhythm that takes 15 minutes every Friday and keeps you from being the last to know when something broke.
  • ✓
    A real cost baseline — you know what your systems cost per month, you know what that's saving you, and you can defend the number if asked.
  • ✓
    One retired workflow — the one that looked impressive but didn't earn its place. Retiring it is the move that proves you're running a portfolio, not collecting builds.
  • ✓
    The muscle memory of reaching for n8n when a task is repeated, rule-shaped, and recoverable — and not reaching for it when the problem is actually a conversation, a decision, or a spreadsheet formula.

That's not a student. That's an operator. And someone who actually runs a small portfolio of AI workflows is — genuinely, in 2026 — ahead of almost every small business they'll encounter. The gap between "I took a course on this" and "I use this daily" is where nearly all the value hides. You're about to be on the right side of it.

A note on the twelve-month view. If you keep this rhythm, a year from today you're probably running 8-12 systems. Monthly cost: under £100. Time saved: 20+ hours a month. Nothing in this plan requires you to be more technical, more determined, or more free than you are right now. It just needs you to not stop.
The one thing to remember

Build, measure, decide, repeat. Fifteen minutes a day for a month. Small, daily, forgiving.

By day 30, you're not a course graduate. You're someone who builds and runs AI systems as part of your normal week. That's the entire gap the plan is designed to close.
