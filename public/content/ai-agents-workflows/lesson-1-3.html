Your stack — three tools, not thirty

Someone on LinkedIn will try to sell you a $5,000 agent-building framework this week. You don't need it. You need three things, and two of them you probably already have.

Before we build anything, let's settle the stack question. The AI agent space looks overwhelming from the outside — dozens of frameworks, hundreds of tools, endless "you must learn X" advice. For a non-coder building real systems in your own business, almost none of it applies. The short list is very short.

!
The common misconception

"You need a complicated stack — LangChain, vector databases, custom code — to build anything serious." That's what developers need when they're building AI products for millions of users. It's not what you need to save yourself four hours a week. You need an orchestrator, a brain, and the business tools you already use.

That's the whole architecture. n8n is the wiring — it holds the workflow together, moves data between steps, and calls the other services when needed. The LLM is the reasoning engine — it classifies, drafts, summarises, decides. Your existing business tools are where the work actually lives and where results go.

Nine out of ten business automations you'll ever want to build are this shape. The ratio of time you'll spend on each layer in this course: about 60% on n8n, 25% on prompt-writing for the LLM, 15% on the business tool integrations.

If you're already happy in Make or Zapier for non-AI stuff, keep them for that. For the three builds in this course, we're using n8n. You'll thank yourself six months in when your systems are running cheaply instead of quietly burning through operations.

Why Claude (with OpenAI as a valid alternative)

The brain layer is where the actual reasoning happens. Both Claude and OpenAI's GPT family work well for the builds in this course. Honest answer on which to pick:

  • Claude (Anthropic) — strong default for agent work. Consistent tool-use, clean structured outputs, less drift over long chains of reasoning. claude-sonnet-4-6 is the sweet spot on cost vs. quality.
  • OpenAI (GPT family) — also fine. Larger ecosystem, slightly better at image-adjacent tasks. GPT-5.4 standard is a good match for most of what we'll build.

Here's the craft-first truth: in 2026, the top frontier models are within a hair of each other on benchmarks. The choice matters less than the system you build around the model. Pick one, use it for every build in this course, and switch later only if you have a concrete reason. I recommend Claude for its consistency in multi-step work, but either will get you to the same place.

A useful frame

Arguing about Claude vs. GPT is like arguing about DeWalt vs. Makita drills. Both are excellent. A professional picks one, learns it well, and stops thinking about it. An amateur keeps switching and wonders why nothing is getting built.

What you don't need (yet, or ever)

Half the confusion in this space comes from courses that teach you tools you don't yet need. Here's what to deliberately skip.

Skip these for now
  • LangChain / LlamaIndex — Python frameworks for developers. n8n handles the same jobs visually, no code.
  • CrewAI / AutoGen — multi-agent frameworks. You're not there yet. Master single-agent first (Module 4 explains why).
  • Vector databases — useful for advanced retrieval. A Google Sheet works for everything we'll build in this course.
  • Custom Python wrappers — if you're reading this, you don't want to maintain them.
  • A dozen LLM subscriptions — one API account is enough. Don't pay for three you're not using.

None of these tools are bad. They're just not what a non-coder needs to ship their first three systems. Every hour spent learning LangChain before you've built anything is an hour not spent actually building. Add complexity later if a specific problem demands it — not before.

The one thing to remember

Three tools, not thirty. n8n, an LLM, and your existing business apps.

This stack handles 95% of what you'll ever want to build. The rest is craft — and craft takes practice, not more tools.
