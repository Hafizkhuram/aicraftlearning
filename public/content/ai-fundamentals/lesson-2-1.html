AI Fundamentals: From Zero to Confident How AI actually works

How LLMs actually predict words

If you've ever wondered why AI sounds so confident when it's wrong, this is the answer.

Module 1 gave you the big picture: AI predicts, it doesn't know. This lesson zooms in. What does "predict" actually mean, mechanically, when you type a question and press Enter? Once you see it, a whole pile of weird AI behaviour stops being weird.

You don't need any maths. You just need to watch prediction happen, one word at a time.

!
The common misconception

"LLMs understand what they're saying." They really don't. They're extremely fast autocomplete systems that have seen billions of sentences. There's no little person inside the machine nodding along to your question. There's just statistics, running at speed.

Autocomplete, on an extraordinary scale

You already use a word-prediction system every day. When you type "I'll be there in five" into your phone and it suggests "minutes" — that's a tiny prediction model. It has seen enough messages to know "minutes" is the most likely next word.

An LLM is the same idea, scaled up beyond recognition. It has read the rough equivalent of a modest national library — billions of web pages, articles, books, and conversations. That reading taught it the statistical patterns of language at every level. Which word tends to follow which, in which context, in which tone, in which register.

When you type a question, the LLM doesn't look anything up. It generates a response one word at a time, each word chosen because it's statistically likely given everything that came before it — your question, plus whatever words it has just written.

Notice something important: every single word is a guess. Even the "obvious" ones. The only reason the output reads fluently is that each guess is informed by vast amounts of text where similar sentences appeared before. At no point does the model stop, look up a fact, or consider whether what it's writing is true. It just picks the next plausible word, over and over, until it decides the response is done.

Why this explains almost everything weird about AI

Once prediction is the mental model, a long list of strange AI behaviours suddenly becomes obvious.

Why does it sound so confident when it's wrong?

Because confidence is a pattern. Textbooks and articles tend to sound assured, so the predicted words do too. The voice is cosmetic.

Why do two people get different answers from the same prompt?

Because at each step there are several plausible next words, and the model picks from them with a sprinkle of randomness. Same input, slightly different path.

Why is it terrible at exact maths?

Because it's predicting what a sum looks like, not calculating it. "2 + 2 = 4" is memorised. Anything uncommon, it invents a plausible-looking answer.

Why does it invent citations and facts?

Because a citation is a pattern too — a shape it has seen thousands of times. It will produce that shape even when no real source exists to fill it.

None of these are bugs that can be patched. They're direct consequences of how the thing works. Prediction is brilliant at generating fluent, plausible language. It is structurally incapable of guaranteeing truth.

The one thing to remember

Every word AI writes is a prediction. Not a fact. Not a belief. A prediction.

Hold this in your head and you'll stop expecting the model to behave like a person who's read the material and formed a view. It didn't, and it hasn't.

Why this matters the moment you press Enter

This isn't abstract. The prediction model has practical consequences for how you should treat AI output, every single time.

When your AI drafts an email that sounds like you, or summarises a thread in three clean bullets, prediction is working with you. Those are shapes it has seen millions of times. The output is fluent because the patterns are strong, and the content is usually fine because language patterns are what the model is best at.

When your AI tells you that your top-selling product last quarter was the X900 earning £47,200, prediction is working against you. It produces something shaped like a real answer — a product name, a number, a currency — because it has seen millions of sentences in that shape. Whether any of those specific words are correct is a completely separate question, and one the model has no way to check.

The takeaway isn't "don't use AI." It's this: treat fluent output and correct output as two different things. They often coincide. They are not the same.

◆ Try this right now

Watch prediction go wrong, on purpose

Open ChatGPT (or Claude, or Gemini) and paste in this exact prompt. It's designed to push the model into confidently inventing something.

List three academic papers on the productivity effects of four-day work weeks, with authors, journal names, and years. Be specific.

Read the response. It will look flawless — real-sounding authors, respectable journals, plausible years. Now try to verify one of them. Google the title. Search the journal. Odds are high that at least one citation is partially or entirely invented. That's prediction doing what prediction does.
