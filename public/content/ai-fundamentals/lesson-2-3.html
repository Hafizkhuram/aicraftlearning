AI Fundamentals: From Zero to Confident How AI actually works

Why outputs feel generic, and the fix

Nine out of ten complaints about AI come down to one thing: generic outputs. Here's why, and the fix.

If you've ever typed "write a product description" into ChatGPT and read back something that could have been written by anyone, about anything, you already know the problem. The output is technically fine. It's also forgettable. It could belong to any business. It sounds like marketing copy from 2016.

This happens to everyone. And it has one cause, and one fix. Neither is a clever trick. Both are hiding in plain sight.

!
The common misconception

"My outputs are bad because I need a better prompting technique." Sometimes, sure. But most of the time the problem isn't the technique. It's that you gave the AI nothing to work with. Generic in, generic out.

Why outputs sound like they were written by nobody

Think back to Lesson 2.1. An LLM predicts the most likely next words. "Most likely" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. If you type a bare prompt — "write a product description" — the model has almost nothing to pin its predictions to. So it predicts the most likely words for the most generic version of that request.

The result is an output that's average in the literal sense: it averages across every product description the model has ever seen. Amazon listings, grocery labels, Etsy bios, Apple marketing. All stirred together. Technically fluent, emotionally flat. It sounds like everyone because it's aimed at no one.

This isn't a flaw in the AI. It's the AI doing exactly what you asked. You asked for an average, so you got one.

The scenario

Jo runs a small leather-goods shop in Bristol. She makes hand-stitched weekender bags for people who want a nicer version of what they've seen in airports. Today she needs a 50-word product description for her new Highbury weekender.

The prompt 7 words
Write a 50-word product description.
What AI produces

Introducing our premium weekender bag, crafted with quality materials and designed for the modern traveller. Perfect for short trips and weekend getaways, this versatile bag combines style and functionality. With ample storage and a sleek design, it's the ideal companion for your next adventure.

!
Fluent, but generic Could describe any bag on earth. No mention of leather, hand-stitching, Bristol, or anything that sounds like Jo. It's the average of every product description the model has ever seen.
The prompt 71 words · same task, with context
I run a small leather-goods shop in Bristol making hand-stitched weekender bags. My customers are people in their 30s and 40s who want a nicer version of what they'd find in an airport — understated, built to last, not shouty. My voice is warm but direct, never salesy. Write a 50-word product description for the Highbury weekender: full-grain English leather, solid brass hardware, fits three nights of kit.
What AI produces

The Highbury is a weekender for people who've done the plastic-handle thing and want better. Full-grain English leather, solid brass hardware, hand-stitched in our Bristol workshop. Roomy enough for three nights away, small enough to sling over one shoulder. Built so you'll hand it down, not throw it out.

✓
Specific, earned, hers Same model, same task. Different prompt. The context gave it audience, voice, and detail to work with — so it predicted words for Jo's description, not a generic one.

This is the whole lesson in one picture. The bad output isn't wrong. It's just aimed at no one. The good output is aimed at Jo's customer, in Jo's voice, with Jo's details. The only thing that changed was what the model had to work with.

The four ingredients of context

When we say "add context," we don't mean write a novel. We mean answer four simple questions before you get to the task. Most good prompts include three or four of these. Most bad prompts include none.

1

Who you are

What do you do, what's your business?

"I run a small leather-goods shop in Bristol…"

2

Who it's for

Who is the audience or reader?

"…customers in their 30s and 40s, want nicer than airport tat…"

3

The tone or voice

How should it sound?

"…warm but direct, never salesy…"

4

The specifics of the task

What exactly do you want, and what are the details?

"…50-word description for the Highbury: full-grain leather, brass hardware, fits three nights."

You don't need all four every time. A quick rewording doesn't need a full biography. But any task where the output needs to sound like you, or land with your audience, lives or dies by these four.

The one thing to remember

Bad output usually isn't an AI problem. It's a context problem.

Before blaming the model, ask: did I give it enough to know who this is for, what tone it needs, and what matters about this task?

Why this is the most useful skill in the course

Prompting technique is genuinely a thing. There are frames and patterns (we'll hit one next module) that structure your prompts in useful ways. But technique is the sprinkle on top. Context is the meal.

Here's what that means in practice. Most of the people who complain that AI "doesn't work for my business" are pasting in nine-word prompts and getting back the average of the internet. They try a new tool, same result. They read a prompt guide, pick up some tricks, get a slightly better result. They never cross the gap that context closes in a single paragraph.

Everything else in this course — the ROLE/CONTEXT/TASK pattern in Module 3, knowing when to trust output, not wasting time on the wrong tasks — sits on top of this one skill. Miss this, and the rest doesn't compound. Get this, and everything else starts working the way you hoped AI would.

Remember the one-paragraph personal brief from Lesson 2.2? That's context ready to paste. You now have half the job done before you've even typed the task.
