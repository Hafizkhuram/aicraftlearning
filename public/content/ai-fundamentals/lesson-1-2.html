AI Fundamentals: From Zero to Confident What AI actually is (and isn't)

The family tree of AI terms

Every AI vendor uses the word "AI" to mean something different. Here's how to cut through the noise with a single mental picture.

Walk into any meeting in 2026 and you'll hear AI, machine learning, deep learning, LLM, and GPT thrown around as if they're the same thing. They aren't. They're related — but in a specific way that, once you see it, you'll never un-see.

This lesson gives you that picture. Once you have it, you'll be the person in the room who can gently ask, "Sorry, when you say AI, which kind do you mean?" and look completely reasonable doing it.

!
The common misconception

"AI is all the same stuff." It isn't. A spam filter and ChatGPT are both called "AI," but they work in completely different ways. Treating them as interchangeable is why people get talked into buying things they don't need.

Four words, nested like Russian dolls

Here's the single most useful picture in AI. Four terms, sitting inside each other like a set of Russian dolls.

AI is the outer doll — the broadest term. Inside it sits Machine Learning. Inside that sits Deep Learning. And inside that sits a particular kind of model called a Large Language Model — an LLM. That's the box Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini all live in.

This matters because every LLM is an AI, but not every AI is an LLM. When a vendor says "our product uses AI," they could be talking about something as sophisticated as GPT-4 or something as simple as a rule-based filter from 1998. Both qualify.

Large Language Models (LLMs)

A specific type of deep learning model — the one behind today's AI boom

Trained on vast amounts of text to predict language. When someone says "AI" in 2026, this is almost always what they mean. It's what you're using when you open Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini.

What each layer actually is

AI is the umbrella. It's any system that does something we'd normally call "intelligent" — spotting spam, recommending films, beating you at chess, writing a product description. The term is over seventy years old and covers everything from hand-written rules to the latest models. It's broad on purpose, which is why it's nearly meaningless when a vendor uses it on its own.

Machine learning is a specific way of doing AI — instead of engineers writing the rules, the system works them out by looking at lots of examples. Show it 10,000 emails labelled "spam" and 10,000 labelled "not spam," and it learns the pattern itself. Your bank uses ML to spot fraud. Netflix uses it to guess what you'll watch next. Nothing magical, just pattern-finding at scale.

Deep learning is a particular flavour of machine learning that uses something called a neural network — loosely inspired by how neurons in a brain are wired together. You don't need to know the maths. You just need to know it's the technique behind most of the impressive AI you've seen in the last decade. Image recognition, voice assistants, and image generators all run on deep learning.

LLMs sit at the innermost layer. They're deep learning models trained specifically on huge amounts of text. They predict language — word by word, sentence by sentence — which is why they're so good at writing, summarising, and rephrasing. Everything you've heard about "AI writing emails" or "AI doing your admin" is almost certainly an LLM doing the work.

The 2026 shortcut everyone uses

Here's the real-world bit. In practice, when somebody at work says "AI," they almost always mean an LLM. The distinction between ML, deep learning, and LLMs has quietly collapsed in everyday conversation, because LLMs are what made the current boom feel like magic.

So your working rule is simple. Nine times out of ten, when someone says "we're using AI for X," they mean they're using an LLM. The tenth time, it's something else — a recommendation engine, a fraud detector, an image classifier — and that's when you should ask which kind.

The one thing to remember

When someone says "AI" in 2026, 90% of the time they mean an LLM. The other 10% you need to ask.

This one question — "which kind of AI?" — will save you from buying tools that don't do what you think they do.

The AI that isn't an LLM

It's worth seeing the other 10% clearly, because these tools are everywhere and they're useful. They're just not what ChatGPT is.

The pattern worth noticing: when the job is language, it's almost always an LLM. When the job is something else — ranking, classifying, recognising, generating images — it's a different flavour of AI underneath. Same umbrella, different tool.

Why this matters when money is involved

Here's where this becomes practical. A software vendor says, "Our CRM is AI-powered." That sentence, on its own, tells you nothing. It could mean:

They've wired up GPT-4 to draft replies to your emails. Or they have a simple ML model that scores leads. Or they renamed a filter from 2015 and slapped "AI" on the label. All three are technically true. All three are very different products at very different prices.

The single question that cuts through it: "Which kind of AI, exactly, and what does it do on a Tuesday afternoon?" If the vendor can't answer that clearly, you now know to be sceptical. If they can — "it's an LLM that drafts email replies based on the customer's history" — then you can judge whether it's worth the money.
