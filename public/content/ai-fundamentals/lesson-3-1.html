The ROLE / CONTEXT / TASK pattern

If you learn only one prompting pattern, make it this one.

Module 2 showed you that context is the meal and technique is the sprinkle. This lesson is the best sprinkle money can buy. It's a three-part structure that outperforms 90% of what people type into AI tools — and it costs you about twenty extra seconds a prompt.

The pattern isn't clever. It's not a secret. It's just a habit of organising what you'd already want to say, in a way that lines up neatly with how LLMs work.

!
The common misconception

"Better prompts need clever phrasing." Not really. Better prompts need better structure. The three-part ROLE / CONTEXT / TASK pattern isn't about magic words — it's about making sure all three pieces actually arrive.

The three parts, at a glance

Every good prompt for a language task answers three questions in order. Give the model a role to play, a situation to understand, and a job to do. That's it.

① Role

Who is the AI being, for this task?

"You are a senior marketing strategist who's worked with UK small businesses for ten years."

② Context

What's the situation? Who is this for? What's the tone?

"I run a small leather-goods shop in Bristol. Customers are late-30s, design-conscious. Voice: warm, direct."

③ Task

What exactly do you want, with what specifics?

"Draft a 50-word product description for the Highbury weekender — full-grain leather, brass hardware, fits three nights."

Notice the pattern mirrors the four ingredients of context from Lesson 2.3, but with one addition: the role line. That line is surprisingly powerful. Telling the model who to be tunes the voice and the assumptions it brings to the whole response. "You are a senior marketing strategist" pulls in a different tone, vocabulary, and default framing than "You are a helpful assistant."

Three separate sentences, each doing a distinct job. The role tunes the voice. The context tells the model who this is for and how it should land. The task gives it the specific thing to produce. Remove any one and the output gets noticeably worse.

Why this particular pattern works

Think back to how LLMs predict words. They generate based on what's in the context window — and a clearly structured prompt gives every word the model produces more to pin to. ROLE sets the voice patterns. CONTEXT sets the audience and tone patterns. TASK sets the goal and the specifics. Each one narrows the probability space toward your version of the output, not the internet's average.

Two other things make this pattern stick. First, it's short enough to remember under pressure. You don't need a prompt library; you just need R · C · T. Second, it reads cleanly to the model — LLMs have seen millions of prompts with "You are a…" openings in their training data, so that role line is a strong signal to them.

When to use it — and when not to

This pattern earns its keep on any serious language task. But it's overkill for quick questions. Here's the rough line.

Use ROLE / CONTEXT / TASK when
Skip it when
Drafting anything someone else will read (emails, posts, descriptions, proposals)
Asking a quick question ("what's the capital of Peru?")
The output needs to sound like you or your business
Brainstorming informally, where a clean, unopinionated list is fine
Summarising or rewriting with a specific audience in mind
Doing casual back-and-forth mid-conversation (the model already has the context)

The rule of thumb: if the output is a deliverable, use the pattern. If the output is a conversation, a casual prompt is fine. Most people under-use the pattern because they treat everything like a conversation. Most disappointment comes from treating deliverables like casual questions.

The one thing to remember

Role. Context. Task. Every time. It's the difference between "okay output" and "actually useful output."

Three sentences. Three jobs. Twenty seconds of effort. It's the single highest-leverage habit in the course.

A note on stacking this with the personal brief

Remember the one-paragraph personal brief from Lesson 2.2? It covers the first half of the CONTEXT line — who you are and your tone — before you've even started typing. So a typical real-world prompt looks like: paste the brief, add a role line, add the situation-specific context, then the task. You're writing less each time, not more, and still hitting all three parts.

That's what craft looks like in practice. Not cleverer prompts. Reused building blocks that let you move fast without losing quality.

Rewrite one real task into the pattern

Think of one task you'd genuinely send to AI in the next day or two. Write the one-line version first. Then rewrite it using ROLE / CONTEXT / TASK.

ROLE
You are ________________________________

CONTEXT
I'm / We're ____________________________
The audience is ________________________
The tone I need is _____________________

TASK
Please draft __________________________
Length / format: _______________________
Key details: ___________________________

If you want the fastest feedback loop: paste the one-line version into ChatGPT, then paste the structured version into a new chat. Compare the two outputs side by side. That's the lesson, proven on your own work.
