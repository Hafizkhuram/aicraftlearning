AI Fundamentals: From Zero to Confident How AI actually works

The context window, and why AI "forgets"

Ever wondered why ChatGPT seems to forget yesterday's conversation? It's not a bug. It's the single most useful thing to understand about how these tools actually work.

You've had the experience. You had a great chat with ChatGPT on Monday. You open it on Wednesday to continue, and it has no idea what you're talking about. You reference "the email we wrote about the supplier issue" and it politely pretends to remember.

The mechanism behind this is simple, and once you know it, a handful of everyday AI frustrations stop being mysterious. You'll also stop making the mistake that wastes most people's time.

!
The common misconception

"AI has a memory, it just keeps forgetting." Almost. It has a working memory — called the context window — and a very specific kind of amnesia. Nothing lives between conversations. Nothing carries over unless you carry it over yourself.

The context window, in plain English

The context window is the patch of text the AI can actually see while it's replying to you. That's it. That's the whole thing. Everything it's about to "know" in this moment has to be inside that window.

Think of it as a clipboard. At any given moment, the clipboard holds four kinds of thing: your current message, the previous back-and-forth in this chat, any documents you've pasted in, and a short set of instructions the app adds behind the scenes (telling the model how to behave). When the model generates its next reply, it reads the clipboard, predicts word by word, and sends you the result.

When you close the chat or start a new one, the clipboard is wiped. Nothing transfers. The model that replies to your Wednesday question is, as far as memory is concerned, the same model meeting you for the first time.

The analogy that makes it stick

Imagine a brilliant consultant with excellent short-term focus but no long-term memory. In a meeting, they're sharp — they pick up everything you say, read every document you hand them, follow every thread of the conversation. But the moment the meeting ends, it's all gone. Next meeting, same story. Perfect in the room, clean slate next time.

That's your AI tool. Every new chat is a new meeting. Whatever you want it to know has to be brought into the room with you.

The numbers, briefly

Modern context windows are large, but they are not infinite. The rough picture in 2026:

~8K

GPT-3.5, older free tiers

Around 6,000 words. Fine for short chats, cramped for long documents.

~128K

GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet

Around 96,000 words. Handles most business documents comfortably.

~200K+

Claude Opus, frontier tier

Around 150,000 words. A short novel, or an entire quarter's emails.

Big, but not unlimited. If you're pasting in a 300-page PDF and then having a long conversation about it, you can still run out of room. When that happens, the tool quietly drops the oldest content. You might notice the AI "forgetting" an instruction you gave thirty messages ago. Now you'll know why.

The one thing to remember

Every new chat is a new brain. Nothing carries over unless you bring it.

If it matters to the conversation, paste it in. If it matters across conversations, save it somewhere outside the AI.

What this changes in how you work

Two practical habits follow from this. Both are small, both save hours.

Keep related work in one chat. If you're drafting a sequence of emails to the same supplier, do it all in one conversation. The model's grasp of the thread stays tight, the tone stays consistent, and you don't have to re-explain the context each time. When the topic changes, start a new chat — it genuinely helps.

Keep a brief for the stuff you paste often. If you find yourself explaining the same business context to the AI every few days — "I run a small consultancy, my clients are mostly mid-market manufacturers, my tone is direct but warm" — save that as a short text file on your desktop. Paste it at the start of any important chat. Three lines at the top gives the model more to work with than most people ever hand over.

Neither habit requires you to be clever. They just require you to know the model has no memory, so you're the memory.

◆ Try this right now

Write your one-paragraph brief

Take ninety seconds. Write three to five sentences about your work that you'd want any AI to know before helping you. It doesn't need to be polished — you'll refine it over weeks.

I run / work at: ____________________
My audience / clients are: ____________________
The tone I usually need is: ____________________
The tools / platforms I use most: ____________________

Save it somewhere you can grab it in two clicks — Notes, a sticky note, a text file called "ai-brief." From now on, paste this at the start of any important chat. Watch the quality of replies shift.
