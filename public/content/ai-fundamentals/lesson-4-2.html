Privacy, and what NOT to paste into AI

Business owners regularly paste things into ChatGPT that would horrify their lawyers. Here's how to stop.

This is the single most important non-technical lesson in the course. Not because the rules are complicated — they aren't. But because a moment's carelessness here can cost you a client, a contract, or real money.

The good news is the mental model is very simple. Once you have it, you'll catch yourself before you paste the wrong thing, and you'll know when reaching for a different tool is the right move.

!
The common misconception

"It's a private conversation between me and the AI." It really isn't. Consumer AI tools keep what you paste, and in many cases it can be used to improve future versions of the model. Treat the chat box like a public forum you happen to be typing into alone.

What actually happens to what you paste

Here's the honest picture, simplified. When you paste text into a consumer AI tool, three things can happen to it:

One — it sits in your chat history. That's the benign part. You can re-open the chat, reference it, delete it.

Two — it's stored on the provider's servers. Also normal. They need to store it to serve it back to you, troubleshoot bugs, enforce their usage policies, and comply with legal requests if they come up.

Three — on free and consumer tiers, it may be used to train future models. This is the bit most people don't realise. Unless you've explicitly turned off training data use in settings (possible on most tools) or you're on a business tier, your conversations may quietly become training material. That training doesn't put your exact words into the next model — but you shouldn't bet anything important on that.

The practical upshot: assume what you paste doesn't stay private. It's not quite as bad as posting on a public forum, but it's closer to that than to a private document on your own computer.

Three tiers — safe, risky, dangerous

Here's the mental model to actually carry around. Three tiers, sorted by the damage a leak would do.

✓

Safe

Public info, your own drafts, general questions

  • Marketing copy you'd publish anyway
  • Blog drafts you wrote
  • Public product descriptions
  • General "how do I…" questions
  • Pasting your own personal brief from Lesson 2.2
~

Risky

Business data, internal docs, identifiable client info

  • Real client names or companies
  • Internal strategy or pricing documents
  • Employee performance notes
  • Business financials and forecasts
  • Unpublished contracts or proposals
!

Dangerous

Credentials, regulated data, other people's private information

  • Passwords or API keys
  • Credit card or bank details
  • Medical or health records
  • Someone else's personal data (GDPR-regulated)
  • Anything covered by NDA

The tiers escalate on two axes: how much damage a leak would do, and who it would affect. Safe stuff affects only you and wouldn't harm anyone if it ended up somewhere public. Dangerous stuff could cost you a regulator fine, a client relationship, or real money — and often affects someone who never agreed to have their information pasted anywhere.

You're drafting a new landing page and want AI help.

"Here's the rough copy I wrote for my landing page — can you tighten it and suggest a better headline? [pasted: your own 300 words of draft copy about your services]"
Safe — fine to paste Your own marketing copy, intended for the public anyway. Nothing private in it, nothing about anyone else. This is exactly what the tool is built to help with.

A supplier sent a late delivery, and you want to draft a response.

"Help me write a firm but professional reply to this supplier about a late shipment: From: sam@blackwood-leather.co.uk Date: 14 March Subject: Order 2847 — delivery delay Hi — just a note that your order (£4,200) has slipped by ten days because our tannery…"
Risky — strip and rephrase first Real supplier name, real email address, real order value. None of it is regulated, but it is identifiable business data belonging to another party. The fix: describe the situation in general terms ("my supplier is 10 days late on a £4K order") and keep the names and email out.

You want help organising your recent card transactions into categories.

"Categorise these transactions by expense type: Amex card ending 4127, April statement Card number: 3712 XXXX XXXX 4127 01/04 — Stripe £340.12 02/04 — Canva £19.00 03/04 — Patient: J. Harrington, balance £1,200 (medical invoice) …"
Dangerous — multiple red lines at once Partial card number (even masked versions are sensitive), a named patient with a medical invoice (health data about someone else), full dates. Any one of these is a stop sign; together they're a pile-up. The categorisation task belongs in a spreadsheet. AI can help describe trends once you've done it there.

You're exploring ideas for a new product line.

"I run a small UK leather-goods shop making weekender bags. I'm thinking about adding a backpack to the range. What would be good design angles to explore, given my existing customer is 30s/40s, design-conscious, and wants understated quality?"
Safe — this is exactly the right way to share business context You've described your business in general terms — no customer names, no unpublished financials, no identifiable individuals. This is the personal brief from Lesson 2.2 doing its job. Share context, not data.

Your login isn't working and you want help troubleshooting.

"My deployment script keeps failing. Here's my env file — can you tell me what's wrong? AWS_ACCESS_KEY_ID=AKIAIOSFODNN7EXAMPLE AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY=wJalrXUtnFEMI/K7MDENG/bPxRfiCYEXAMPLEKEY DATABASE_URL=postgres://admin:Str0ngP@ss!@db.mycompany.com:5432/…"
Dangerous — rotate those keys immediately if pasted Real API keys and database passwords. If those ever leak, someone can use them directly against your infrastructure. Redact every credential before pasting anything technical — replace each one with something like YOUR_AWS_KEY_HERE. If you've already pasted the real ones, rotate the keys now, not later.

The one rule to carry everywhere

You won't remember the three tiers under pressure. What you will remember — if you drill it enough times — is a single sentence you can apply in a second. Here it is.

The paste rule

"Don't paste anything you wouldn't email to a stranger."

If you'd hesitate to send it to someone you don't know, don't paste it into a consumer AI tool. That single question catches almost every mistake.

The beauty of the rule is that it outperforms memorising GDPR, NDAs, or training data policies. You already know what you'd email to a stranger — essentially nothing personal, nothing confidential, nothing about anyone else. Apply the same filter to AI and you'll be fine more than 99% of the time.

When you genuinely need to share sensitive data

Sometimes you really do need AI help with business-sensitive material — a contract to review, client notes to summarise, a financial model to sanity-check. The answer isn't to stop using AI. It's to use a tier designed for it.

Paid business or team tiers — ChatGPT Team/Business, Claude for Work, Google Workspace Gemini — typically commit in writing not to use your data for training. That's a real, contract-backed difference from the consumer tiers. They're a sensible spend for anyone regularly handling confidential material.

Anonymise before pasting. For one-off jobs where a paid tier isn't available, strip the identifiers. Replace real names with "the client," real companies with "Company A," real figures with "~£500K." The AI doesn't need the real names to help you draft or analyse — it just needs the structure.

For code and technical content. Never paste real API keys, passwords, or secrets. Replace every credential with a placeholder like YOUR_API_KEY. Most AI tools will give you exactly the same help without the real values.

The one thing to remember

Your AI's training set is not a private conversation. Treat it accordingly.

Don't paste anything you wouldn't email to a stranger. For genuinely sensitive work, use a business-tier tool or anonymise before you hit send.
