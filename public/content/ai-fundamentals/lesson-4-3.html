How to spot AI hype and misinformation

There are more AI-powered products advertised than actually exist. Here's how to tell them apart.

You've made it to the final lesson. You now know how AI works, what it's good at, how to prompt it properly, and what not to paste into it. This lesson does the opposite: it trains you to spot the nonsense so you can walk past it with confidence.

The AI space is saturated with marketing. Some of it describes genuinely useful products. A lot of it describes nothing at all. After this lesson, you'll be able to tell the difference in about fifteen seconds.

!
The common misconception

"If a product mentions AI, it must do something new." It might. Or it might be a 2018 rules engine with a lick of paint. Every CRM, inbox, analytics dashboard, and scheduling app on earth now claims to be AI-powered. The label tells you almost nothing.

The language of AI hype

Marketing language has patterns, like everything else. Once you've seen them a few times, they jump out. Most AI hype leans on a small set of words and phrases that feel impressive but describe nothing concrete.

✕

Red flags — walk past

  • "Autonomous" — does what, exactly?
  • "Revolutionary" or "game-changing"
  • "AGI-powered" or "superintelligent"
  • "No human needed" / "replaces your entire team"
  • "Unlock 10x productivity" (or any specific multiplier)
  • Vague phrases like "leverages advanced AI"
  • Testimonials that don't describe what happened
✓

Green flags — pay attention

  • A specific, concrete use case
  • Honest statements about what it can't do
  • Realistic pricing with clear tiers
  • Before/after examples with actual output
  • Names the underlying model (e.g. Claude or GPT-4o)
  • Time savings expressed as a range, not a promise
  • Humans still visible in the workflow

The rule of thumb: if a vendor can't tell you what their product actually does, neither can you. And if you can't describe it to a colleague in one concrete sentence, you probably shouldn't be paying for it.

The single question that cuts through it all

You don't need a checklist for most AI marketing. You need one question, applied consistently. It's this.

The one question

"What exactly would this do on Monday morning?"

Ask it of any AI product, pitch, or tool. If the answer is vague, you've got a hype product. If the answer is a specific task someone would actually do, you've got a real one.

Notice how the question forces specificity. "Revolutionary sales AI" doesn't survive it — what does it do on Monday morning? "Drafts follow-up emails to cold leads based on last call's transcript" does survive it. One is a product. The other is a pitch deck.

The question also keeps you honest about what you'd actually use. A Monday-morning task you'd never do anyway is still a bad fit — however real the product is. The filter works in both directions.

Seen on LinkedIn, sponsored post

"Our AI-powered platform leverages cutting-edge neural intelligence to revolutionise your entire go-to-market motion and unlock 10x productivity."

Hype — four red flags in one sentence "Leverages," "cutting-edge," "revolutionise," "10x productivity" — and no mention of what the product actually does on any given Monday. This is the pure form of AI hype: dense with promise, empty of specifics.

SaaS landing page, pricing section

"Drafts personalised follow-up emails for your sales team, using the last call transcript plus the contact's history from your CRM. Claude 3.5 Sonnet under the hood. £29/user/month. Saves most teams 2–5 hours per rep per week."

Real — everything a real product looks like Concrete Monday-morning task (drafts follow-up emails). Names the model. Realistic pricing. Time savings as a range, not a promise. You know exactly what you're buying in a sentence.

Press release

"Industry-first autonomous AI employee that replaces your entire operations team. Zero human oversight required. Deploys in minutes."

Hype — and probably a liability "Autonomous," "replaces your entire team," "zero human oversight" — hype flag triple-stack. Worth remembering Lesson 3.2: AI is your fastest intern, not your advisor. Anyone selling zero-oversight AI is either confused, exaggerating, or about to get someone into serious trouble.

Product docs, new feature announcement

"New feature: Meeting summariser. Paste a meeting transcript, get a structured summary with decisions made, action items with owners, and open questions. Does not replace proper minutes for regulated industries — we recommend a human reviewer.

Real — and unusually honest Very specific Monday-morning task. Named inputs, named outputs. Explicit limitation ("does not replace proper minutes for regulated industries"). Honest caveats are a strong trust signal — a vendor willing to say what their tool isn't usually has a clearer view of what it is.

Conference keynote

"By 2026, AGI will transform every job on Earth. Those who don't adopt our AGI-native workflow today will be left behind in 18 months."

Hype — and using fear as the close "AGI," vague timelines, "left behind" urgency. No product description at all, just a threat. When the pitch is fear rather than function, the question "what does it do on Monday morning?" hits empty — which tells you exactly what it's worth.

Instagram ad

"I made £34,000 in my first month using this AI side-hustle. Anyone can do it. DM me for the full system."

Hype — the oldest trick, AI-branded Specific impressive number, impossible to verify. "Anyone can do it" is a classic unfalsifiable claim. "DM for the system" means no product page, no pricing, no specifics. This is pyramid-scheme marketing with AI as the new gloss.

How to spot AI-generated content too

A small note before the takeaway. The same filter that catches AI hype also catches a lot of AI-generated content masquerading as human writing. The signs are familiar once you know them.

Overused phrases like "in today's rapidly evolving landscape" or "it's crucial to remember that…" Heavy use of tricolons — "empowering, enabling, and enriching." Sentences that average around the same length. A general absence of specifics, names, or concrete examples. Smooth, confident, and somehow saying nothing.

None of these are proof. Humans write this way too, especially tired ones. But a piece of writing that hits three or four of those patterns, with no specific details of any kind, is probably AI output someone didn't bother to edit. Applying the same "what does this actually say on Monday morning?" question usually sorts it out.

The one thing to remember

If you can't describe what the tool does in one concrete sentence, neither can the vendor. Move on.

The AI hype industry runs on vague promises. A single specific question — "what does this do on Monday morning?" — defeats almost all of it.
