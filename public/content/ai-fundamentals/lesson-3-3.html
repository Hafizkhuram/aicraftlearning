AI Fundamentals: From Zero to Confident How to work with AI well

The 3 ways people waste time with AI

If you think AI should be saving you more time than it is, you're probably doing one of these three things.

You now know how AI works, how to write a proper prompt, and when to trust what comes back. The last piece is recognising the patterns of unproductive use — the things that look like using AI but actually drain your week.

There are three. They're predictable. You'll catch yourself doing at least one of them in the next seven days. That's the goal.

!
The common misconception

"Being unproductive with AI means I'm doing prompts wrong." Not usually. It means you've fallen into one of three habits that cost time regardless of how clever your prompts are. The fix isn't better prompting. It's noticing the pattern early and stopping.

The three wastes

01

Waste #1

Tool-hunting instead of answer-hunting

Opening ChatGPT because it's open, not because you have a real problem. Asking it things you already know. Treating the conversation as the task rather than a means to the task.

"Hmm, let me see what ChatGPT thinks about…" — asked about the colour of the sky, the definition of a word, or how to reply to an email you already know how to reply to.

The fix Before you type, state the real outcome you want. "I need a 3-sentence follow-up email for my client by 4pm." If you can't state it, you're tool-hunting.
02

Waste #2

Not iterating

Accepting the first output as if it's the only output. Either using it as-is when it's not quite right, or giving up and writing it from scratch yourself.

First draft comes back. It's 70% there. You sigh, decide AI can't do this, and spend 20 minutes rewriting from scratch. You've just paid for both.

The fix Before rewriting, give one specific piece of feedback. "Make the second paragraph warmer." "Tighten this by 30%." "The opening doesn't sound like me — more conversational." Usually one round of feedback closes the gap.
03

Waste #3

Using AI for the wrong kind of task

Handing AI work it's structurally bad at — exact maths, live facts, precise citations, reliable counting — then getting frustrated when it fails. You already know this from Lesson 1.3, but it takes a while to actually catch yourself doing it.

"Can you add up these 40 invoice totals for me?" — then noticing, ten minutes later, that the sum is off by £340, and wondering why AI can't do basic maths.

The fix Before you ask, sort the task. Is this a language task or a precision task? If precision, use the right tool (a spreadsheet, a search engine, a calculator) and get AI to help describe what you find there.

Diagnose what's happening here

Four short scenarios. For each, work out which waste pattern is in play. This is harder than it looks — most of them have a surface appearance that masks the real issue.

Self-diagnostic · 4 scenarios 0 of 4 diagnosed

Pick which waste pattern fits each scenario. Feedback appears straight away.

Scenario 1

You ask AI to draft a client proposal. The first draft comes back. The structure's right, but the tone is too formal and the third paragraph is wordy. You stare at it for two minutes, then open a blank document and write the whole proposal yourself from scratch.

Waste #2 — not iterating The draft was 70% there. One round of feedback — "make it less formal, tighten paragraph three by a third" — probably closes it in 30 seconds. Rewriting from scratch is the hidden cost of not iterating.

Scenario 2

You paste last quarter's sales CSV into Claude and ask which product line grew fastest. It gives you a confident answer with specific percentages. Later you notice the figures don't match what you get when you actually do the maths in a spreadsheet.

Waste #3 — wrong task type Exact figures from a real dataset is a precision task. AI can help you describe trends once you've done the maths, or help write a summary of findings — but the arithmetic itself belongs in a spreadsheet or a tool with a calculator attached.

Scenario 3

You're procrastinating a hard decision about a hire. You open ChatGPT and type "tell me the pros and cons of hiring a junior vs a senior developer." You read the response. Then ask it to reframe. Then ask the same question a different way. Forty minutes pass. You still haven't made the decision.

Waste #1 — tool-hunting The real problem isn't "what are the pros and cons" — you already know them. The real problem is making a decision that's genuinely yours to make. Chatting with AI feels like progress but isn't. State the outcome ("decide by Friday") and you'll notice the tool can't help much here.

Scenario 4

The AI gives you a LinkedIn post draft that's fine, not great. Instead of asking for changes, you rewrite the prompt from scratch with slightly different wording and ask again. The second draft is also fine. You ask a third time with different words. You end up with six drafts, all okay, none great. Forty minutes gone.

Waste #2 — not iterating Tricky one, because on the surface it looks like heavy engagement with the tool. But rewriting the prompt from scratch six times isn't iterating — it's restarting. Real iteration is staying in the chat and giving specific feedback on the draft you already have ("the opening is too generic — try leading with a concrete example").

The pre-flight check that catches all three

Here's a ten-second habit that catches every one of these before it starts. Before you type the prompt, ask yourself three questions.

The pre-flight check

Ask yourself, before you type

What's the real outcome I want? If you can't name it in a sentence, you're probably tool-hunting.

Is this a language task or a precision task? Precision tasks belong in other tools; ask AI to help around them, not do them.

If the first draft isn't perfect, what specific feedback will I give? Deciding this upfront makes iteration the default instead of a last resort.

Ten seconds. Most of the time you'll type the prompt anyway and move on. But roughly one time in five, you'll catch yourself doing something that was about to cost you half an hour. That catch is where the time savings in this course actually live.

The one thing to remember

Before you type, ask yourself: is this a good use of AI, or am I just tool-hunting?

Most lost time with AI comes from one of three patterns. Naming them is half the fix.

What good use actually looks like

To keep the three wastes from sounding like a list of "don'ts," here's the opposite in a single sentence. Good AI use is short, focused, and iterative on the output — not on the prompt.

Short: you open the chat when you have a clear outcome in mind, and you close it when the outcome is done. Focused: you're doing language work (drafting, summarising, rewriting) or getting help thinking about something, and you've accepted that precision tasks need a different tool. Iterative: you stay in the same chat, give specific feedback on each draft, and let the model build on what's already in its context window.

That's the whole discipline. It's not about clever prompting. It's about staying honest with yourself about what you're actually trying to do.
