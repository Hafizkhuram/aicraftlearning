Building AI Agents & Workflows What you're actually building

Workflows vs. agents — know the difference

Before you build anything, you need to know what you're building. Most failed AI projects blur the line between these two things — and pay for it.

On LinkedIn, agent and workflow get thrown around like they mean the same thing. They don't. And the difference between them is the single most expensive thing to get wrong. By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to look at any task and know — in about thirty seconds — which one you actually need. Nine times out of ten, it's the workflow.

!
The common misconception

"An AI agent and an AI workflow are basically the same thing — different names for automation." They're not. They work differently, cost differently, and fail differently. Getting this wrong is why most AI projects quietly get abandoned three weeks in.

The one-sentence distinction

Here it is: A workflow follows a fixed path. An agent decides what to do next.

A workflow is "if this, then this, then this" — steps you wrote in order. The tool just runs them. Same thing, same order, every time. That's a feature, not a limitation.

An agent is different. It receives a task, looks at what's in front of it, and decides — using an LLM — what to do next. It might check something, then decide whether to check something else, then draft, then loop back for more research. The path isn't fixed. It emerges, one decision at a time, from the agent's reasoning.

A workflow is a recipe. An agent is the cook.

The same problem, solved two ways

Take one real problem — triaging incoming support emails — and see both approaches side by side.

◆ Workflow approach

Sort emails by category, draft standard replies

  • Trigger: a new email arrives in the inbox.
  • Classify the email as urgent, routine, or spam.
  • Apply the matching label, and route to the right folder.
  • For routine emails, draft a reply from a template and save as draft.
◆ Agent approach

Read, reason, and respond (or escalate)

  • Receive the email.
  • Decide: is this urgent? If unsure, check the customer's history.
  • Draft a tailored reply using past threads as context.
  • If the question's outside known patterns, flag for a human.

Both will process your emails. But they're doing very different jobs underneath:

  • The workflow runs your four steps, in order, every time. Predictable. Cheap. Easy to fix.
  • The agent reasons at every step. Every decision is an LLM call. More powerful when the path genuinely varies. Slower, pricier, harder to debug.

Picture a weird email — half complaint, half question, about a product you stopped selling two years ago. The workflow will quietly classify it as "routine" and draft a mediocre reply. Consistent, at least. You'll see it in drafts before it sends. The agent might genuinely do better — check the customer's history, spot the discontinued product, flag for a human. Or it might loop, draft three replies, and burn £4 of API credit deciding what to do. Both outcomes are common. That variance is what you're paying for when you choose an agent.

Why this choice matters more than any other

Most abandoned AI projects fail at this first decision, not at the build. People see an interesting problem, reach for an agent because agents are what the internet is excited about, and spend six weeks on something over-engineered for what they needed. Then they switch it off.

The uncomfortable truth: most business tasks are workflow problems.

Workflow jobs (fixed path): sorting emails, generating invoices, logging meeting notes, sending weekly reports, tagging leads, routing form submissions, reminding people about overdue tasks.

Agent jobs (path genuinely varies): competitive research where you don't know what you'll find, customer-history lookups that span multiple systems, investigation tasks that branch on what they discover.

Then there's the cost story. A workflow sorting 500 emails a month might cost £2 in LLM fees. The same problem solved with an agent — multiple reasoning calls per email — can easily run £40-£50 for no extra value. Simpler is cheaper, and cheaper is what keeps a system running past month three.

A useful test

If you could write the steps on a sticky note in advance, and they'd be the same every Tuesday — workflow. If the right next step genuinely depends on what the system finds along the way — agent.

The one thing to remember

Start with the simplest thing that works. Add intelligence only where you need it.

A workflow you can ship in a day beats an agent you can't debug in a month. Pick the least clever option that gets the job done.

What people get wrong when they choose

Two predictable mistakes. Spot them in yourself before you spend real time on a build.

Mistake one: reaching for an agent because it sounds more impressive. "Agents are the new thing, so I should use an agent." But nobody in your business will ever ask whether your automation has a reasoning loop. They'll ask whether the emails are getting sorted. If a three-step workflow does the job, that's the right answer.

Mistake two: forcing a workflow onto a task that needs judgement. It works in testing, because testing uses the five examples you had in mind. Then real inputs arrive, things don't fit the pattern, and the output is quietly rubbish for a month before you notice. If the path really does need to vary, don't fight it — build an agent, with the guardrails we'll cover later.

Two quick questions settle the decision:

  • Do the steps need to change based on what shows up? No → workflow. Yes → agent.
  • How much does it matter if this goes wrong? The higher the stakes, the more you'll invest in guardrails, error handling, and human checkpoints (covered later).

One last thing: it's not strictly a binary. Plenty of real systems are workflows with a single agent-style step in the middle — a fixed pipeline that hands off to an LLM for one judgement call, then carries on. That's a sensible pattern, and you'll build exactly that in Module 2. But you still need to know which end of the spectrum your task sits closer to, because that drives how you design it.

◆ Try this right now

Audit one task in your own business

Pick a repetitive task in your week — something that eats your time and that you've thought about automating. Write it down in a sentence, then run it through the test.

Task: ____________________________________ Can I write the steps in advance, same every time? → Yes = workflow. → No = agent.

Keep the answer somewhere. You'll come back to this task in Module 2 when we start building. Most of you will land on a workflow. Good — that's the right place to start.
