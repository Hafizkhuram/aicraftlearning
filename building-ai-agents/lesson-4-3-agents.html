Building AI Agents & Workflows Beyond building

Running AI systems over time

Building a system takes a week. Keeping it running takes forever. Plan accordingly — or don't, and discover the hard way that the unglamorous half of the work is where all the real value actually lives.

This is the lesson almost no one teaches. Courses are full of "build your first agent" tutorials; almost none of them come back six months later to say what happened to the agent. The honest answer, for most of them, is that they broke quietly and nobody noticed — because the people who built them never set up a way to notice. This lesson is how you become someone who notices.

!
The common misconception

"Once it's built, it'll just keep working." It won't. The models change under you. The tools change under you. Your business changes around you. Every AI system you've built is sitting on three moving platforms, and the cost of not noticing the drift compounds over weeks and months. The fix isn't complicated — it's three calendar reminders and the discipline to actually honour them.

Three kinds of drift

Your AI systems drift in three different ways, on three different timescales. Knowing which is which is the mental model that makes maintenance tractable.

◆ Model drift

The LLM changes under you

Providers update their models. Claude 4.6 gets replaced by 4.7. Older models get deprecated. Even "the same model" can shift behaviour subtly between versions. Prompts that worked yesterday produce slightly different outputs tomorrow, even with no code change on your side.

Check quarterly
◆ Tool drift

Integrations break at the edges

OAuth tokens expire. API fields get renamed. Rate limits tighten. Google alone has been known to expire personal OAuth tokens after seven days — no error, just a silently failing workflow. The tool you depend on doesn't care if your automation works.

Check monthly
◆ Business drift

Your own needs change

The workflow that triaged 30 emails a day stops making sense at 300. The competitor list that mattered six months ago is half-obsolete. The "routine" email category from Module 2 doesn't match what your inbox looks like anymore.

Review annually

None of these is anyone's fault. It's just what happens when you build on top of a stack that's moving independently of you. The craft skill isn't avoiding drift — it's expecting it, and knowing which timescale each kind sits on.

◆ The 2026 industry reality

Prompts that worked yesterday can fail tomorrow

In 2026, one enterprise team documented their regression test suite — carefully built against one Claude version — dropping to 95.1% pass rate when tested against the next. Nothing had changed in their code. The model's behaviour had shifted subtly, and only an explicit test caught it. Without that test, the degradation would have been invisible until customer complaints surfaced weeks later.

The same year, Google blocked new access to Gemini 2.0 Flash for new projects. Teams who'd built on it had no choice but to migrate. Announcements happen weeks in advance; the teams who survive them are the ones already checking for this kind of news on a regular cadence.

Weekly — the execution review

The highest-leverage habit, and the cheapest. Every Monday morning, spend ten minutes looking at your n8n Executions tab.

Weekly ~10 min · Mondays

What to look at

  • Any red (failed) executions from the past week — open them, skim the error
  • Total run count per workflow — big drop vs the week before usually means a trigger failed silently
  • Runtime drift — if Build #2 used to finish in 90 seconds and is now hitting 6 minutes, something changed
  • The run log sheets from Build #1 and #2 — a 30-second scroll tells you if anything looks off
  • Your kill-switch Sheet — make sure everything is still set to active

Ten minutes. Maybe fifteen if you spot something worth investigating. The difference between this habit and nothing is the difference between catching a broken workflow on Monday and discovering it on Friday when someone asks you why the weekly briefing didn't arrive.

Make the executions tab a tab you open on Monday. Bookmark it. Put it in your browser tab group for work-first-hour. The habit is the entire point — the execution view itself is already doing the hard work of surfacing problems. Your job is to look.

Monthly — costs, credentials, and API changes

Once a month, twenty minutes, a bit more depth. This is where most of the "silent rot" happens in AI systems — the stuff weekly checks don't catch.

Monthly ~20 min · 1st of the month

What to review

  • LLM spend — open Anthropic Console. Compare this month to last. A 2× jump with no new workflows means one of your prompts has drifted into using more tokens, or one of your agents is looping longer. Investigate before it doubles again.
  • Tool spend — SerpAPI usage, any other per-call tools. Same logic. A spike without a clear business reason is worth a look.
  • OAuth credentials — open your n8n credentials list. Look for anything flagged as expired or "reconnection required." Google in particular can silently expire personal-account OAuth tokens weekly. Reconnect proactively rather than waiting for a workflow to fail.
  • Provider announcements — five minutes on Anthropic's news page (or OpenAI's, or whichever you use). Any model deprecations coming up? Any new models worth testing? Put significant announcements in your calendar for the quarterly review.
Honest craft note: OAuth expiry is genuinely annoying. For Google services in particular, personal accounts can expire every 7 days; Google Workspace accounts are more stable. If you run multiple workflows on personal credentials and find yourself reconnecting constantly, it's worth migrating to a Google Workspace account or — for heavier use — a Google service account. Neither is glamorous. Both save real time.

Quarterly — the model and prompt review

Once a quarter, one hour, the deepest pass. This is where you catch model drift before it catches you.

Quarterly ~1 hour · first week of Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct

What to check

  • Output quality spot-check. Pull three real recent outputs from each system. Are they still as good as the ones you remember from when you built it? If any look noticeably worse, the prompt may have drifted — or the model's default behaviour may have shifted.
  • Prompt review. Re-read your system prompts and tool descriptions with fresh eyes. Anything vague? Anything you'd write differently now? Module 2 and 3 craft patterns still apply — this is your chance to polish what you rushed past when you were building.
  • Model version check. Are you still on claude-sonnet-4-6, or is there a newer default worth testing? The Anthropic-recommended default shifts over time. Test the new one against a recent real input before switching.
  • Is this system still worth running? The honest question. Which of your workflows actually saved you time this quarter? Which ones haven't triggered in a month? Retire the ones that aren't earning their cost — more on this below.
When you test a new model version Don't switch without comparing. Take five representative inputs from the past quarter. Run them through the current model and the proposed new one. Compare outputs side by side. Most of the time they'll be similar enough that the newer model is a safe upgrade. Occasionally they won't be — maybe the new model hedges where the old one was direct, or structures JSON slightly differently. Catch that in a planned test, not in a broken production run.

The three calendar reminders

Here's the entire lesson in three calendar events. Add them now, before you close this tab. They take two minutes to set up and they're what separate a working AI stack from a quietly rotting one.

◆ Copy this into your calendar

Three recurring events that save hours of surprise-fixing

W
AI systems — weekly review
Repeats: every Monday at 9:00am · Duration: 15 min

Open n8n Executions tab. Check for red rows, runtime drift, kill-switch status. Skim run logs for Build #1 and Build #2. Write one note if anything's worth revisiting.

M
AI systems — monthly health check
Repeats: 1st of the month · Duration: 30 min

Check LLM spend vs last month. Review OAuth credentials for expiry. Read latest provider announcements (Anthropic news). Reconnect anything that needs it.

Q
AI systems — quarterly review
Repeats: 1st of Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct · Duration: 1 hour

Spot-check recent outputs for quality. Re-read system prompts and tool descriptions. Check for new model versions worth testing. Decide which systems to retire.

Retire with discipline

One final piece of craft that most courses skip: a system you don't use anymore is a system that's still costing you money and attention. Every n8n workflow on an active schedule consumes polling cycles, API calls, and your mental overhead when it errors. Every agent with a live credential is a small risk surface. Every kill-switch row is one more thing to keep an eye on.

Once a quarter, look at each of your systems and ask, honestly: "If I were starting from scratch today, would I build this?" If the answer is no, retire it. Deactivate the workflow. Archive the Sheet. Revoke the credentials it no longer needs. Update your kill-switch tab. This takes five minutes per system and it genuinely removes weight from your operational load.

Retirement is a craft skill, not a failure. Most working AI practitioners run fewer systems over time, not more — because they've learned which ones genuinely earn their cost, and they're confident enough to let the rest go.

The one thing to remember

A system you don't check is a system that's quietly failing. Put it in the calendar.

Three recurring events — weekly 15 min, monthly 30 min, quarterly 1 hour — are the difference between AI systems that keep earning and AI systems that quietly rot. Unglamorous, reliable, and the part that actually separates practitioners from tutorial-finishers.
