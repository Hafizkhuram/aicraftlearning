Building AI Agents & Workflows Build your first workflow

Anatomy of a workflow — triggers, nodes, actions

Every workflow in n8n is made of the same three Lego bricks. Once you see them, the whole interface clicks — and the "hundreds of integrations" stop being scary.

Welcome to Module 2. Module 1 got you set up and gave you the mental model. From here on we're building — and the first thing to understand is the shape of what we're about to build.

This lesson is a cheat sheet. Five minutes, one mental model, and every n8n workflow you ever look at will parse instantly. Including the ones you'll build over the next three lessons.

!
The common misconception

"n8n is complicated — there are hundreds of nodes to learn." There are hundreds of integrations, yes. But they all answer one of three questions: what starts this workflow?, what happens in the middle?, and what does it do at the end? Learn those three questions and the size of the integration list stops mattering.

The three Lego bricks

Every workflow — every single one, no exceptions — is built from these three parts, in this order.

1 Trigger
What starts it

Every workflow begins with one trigger. It's the "when" — the event that wakes the workflow up and kicks it off.

Common triggers A new email arrives. A schedule fires (e.g. every Monday at 7am). A form gets submitted. A file lands in a Drive folder. A webhook receives data.
2 Nodes
What shapes the data

Once triggered, data flows through a series of processing steps. Each one takes input, does something to it, and passes the result along.

Common processing Classify this with Claude. Extract the name and amount. Look something up in Sheets. Reformat the text. Split by category.
3 Actions
What it does at the end

The workflow closes by doing something useful in the outside world — writing a file, sending a message, updating a record.

Common actions Save a draft to Gmail. Append a row to Sheets. Post to Slack. Create a Notion page. Send a webhook.

That's the whole framework. A trigger kicks it off, processing nodes shape the data along the way, and one or more actions finish the job by doing something in the real world.

On the n8n canvas this renders as exactly what you'd expect — four boxes connected by lines, left to right. The first box has a distinctive trigger icon. The last box is an action. The two in the middle are where the thinking happens.

Once you see this shape, it's hard to unsee. Every n8n workflow you ever look at — yours, a template, a screenshot on a forum — resolves into this pattern. Trigger on the left, action on the right, processing in the middle.

Why this makes the interface click

n8n has 500+ integrations. That sounds overwhelming until you realise what's on the list: 500+ specific answers to the same three questions.

  • Gmail in the trigger slot = "start when a new email arrives." In the action slot = "save a draft" or "send a message."
  • Google Sheets as a node = "look something up" or "write a row." As an action = "append data."
  • Claude / OpenAI as a node = "classify this" or "extract that." As an action = almost always not — it's the brain, not the output.
  • Slack as a trigger = "start when someone posts in this channel." As an action = "notify this channel."

The same service often appears in two slots. Gmail can trigger a workflow and be the action at the end. Sheets can both read and write. You're not learning 500 different things — you're learning how four or five tools behave in each of the three slots. And realistically, you'll use the same 6-8 integrations for 95% of what you build.

One small honest footnote In n8n's interface, everything — including triggers and actions — is technically called a "node." The trigger/node/action split in this lesson is a mental model, not n8n's own terminology. But it's a useful one: once you see which slot each node is playing, the canvas stops looking like a blob and starts looking like a sentence.
The one thing to remember

Triggers start it. Nodes shape it. Actions finish it. Every workflow. Every time.

The canvas isn't 500 integrations — it's three slots you fill in. Once you see that, n8n stops being a tool you're fighting and starts being a shape you're filling.

What to watch for when you build

Two patterns to notice as you start looking at workflows — including your own.

Every workflow has exactly one trigger. You can only start once. If you want the same processing to fire from two different sources (say, both a new email and a schedule), that's two workflows, not one with two triggers. This catches people out early.

A workflow can have more than one action. It's very common for a single workflow to end by doing several things at once — save a draft in Gmail and log the classification to Sheets and post a Slack notification. These aren't a series of actions in a line; they're a fan-out at the end.

Once you've built one real workflow — which you'll do in Lesson 2.2 — these will become muscle memory. But even on first read, knowing that a workflow has one start and potentially many finishes will save you a confused five minutes later this week.

◆ Get comfortable with the canvas

Drag in one trigger and one action

Open your n8n workspace. Create a new workflow. Don't worry about making it do anything yet — just get a feel for the canvas.

1. Workflows → Add Workflow 2. Click "+" on the canvas → pick any Trigger (e.g. Schedule Trigger) 3. Click "+" next to the trigger → pick any Action (e.g. Gmail → Send Message) 4. Notice: the trigger has a different icon shape 5. Click around. Drag nodes. Don't save anything.

Five minutes of poking around, and the canvas stops being a mystery. Next lesson we build the real thing.
